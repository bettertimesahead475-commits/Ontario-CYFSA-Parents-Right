Section 7
Life, Liberty and Security of the Person
‚öñÔ∏è
"Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice."
What this means in plain language: The government ‚Äî including CAS ‚Äî cannot take away your child, your freedom, or cause you serious harm unless they follow fair rules and have a legitimate legal reason. This is your most powerful right in a CAS case.

"Principles of fundamental justice" means CAS must:
‚Ä¢ Have a real legal reason for their actions (not just a hunch)
‚Ä¢ Follow a fair process ‚Äî you get to know the allegations and respond
‚Ä¢ Not act in an arbitrary or overbroad way
‚Ä¢ Not cause effects that are grossly disproportionate to any harm
‚úÖ Lawful CAS action: CAS receives a report of visible bruising on a child, investigates, speaks with teachers and doctors, and only removes after gathering evidence showing the child is at risk.
üî¥ Possible Section 7 violation: CAS removes a child based solely on a neighbour's anonymous tip, without any investigation, without speaking to you, and without going to court first.
üí° What to do: Document everything. Ask CAS in writing: "What specific CYFSA s.74(2) grounds are you relying on?" They must answer. If they can't specify a legal ground, that is a Section 7 violation.
Section 8
Search and Seizure
üè†
"Everyone has the right to be secure against unreasonable search or seizure."
What this means: CAS cannot enter your home without one of three things:
‚Ä¢ Your consent (you invite them in ‚Äî you can say no)
‚Ä¢ A warrant (a judge has authorized entry under CYFSA s.81)
‚Ä¢ A genuine emergency (immediate danger to a child's life ‚Äî s.84 CYFSA)

You have the right to say no at your door. You do not have to let CAS into your home. Standing in the doorway and speaking with them is not the same as consent to enter.
‚úÖ What to say at your door: "I am not consenting to you entering my home. If you have a warrant, please show it to me through the door. I am calling a lawyer right now."
üî¥ Possible Section 8 violation: CAS pushes past you into your home without consent, without a warrant, and without an obvious emergency. Any evidence they gather from that search may be challenged in court.
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: If you consent to entry, you waive this right for that visit. Never consent when uncertain. Ask for a lawyer first.
Section 9
Arbitrary Detention or Imprisonment
üö™
"Everyone has the right not to be arbitrarily detained or imprisoned."
What this means: You cannot be held at a CAS office, in your home, or anywhere else against your will without legal authority. Detention includes psychological pressure ‚Äî if you feel you cannot leave a meeting, that may constitute detention.

You can always say: "Am I free to go?" If they say yes, leave. If they say no, ask: "Under what legal authority are you detaining me?"
üî¥ Possible Section 9 violation: A CAS worker tells you that you cannot leave their office until you answer their questions, or blocks your exit, without any lawful authority to detain you.
Section 10(b)
Right to Retain and Instruct Counsel
üë®‚Äç‚öñÔ∏è
"Everyone has the right on arrest or detention to retain and instruct counsel without delay and to be informed of that right."
What this means: The moment you feel detained or compelled to answer CAS questions ‚Äî especially about your child's safety ‚Äî you have the right to speak with a lawyer first. You should say this immediately.

Legal Aid Ontario provides free duty counsel who can advise you over the phone before any interview. Call: 1-800-668-8258
‚úÖ What to say to CAS: "I want to speak with a lawyer before I answer any questions. I am invoking my right to counsel. Please give me the opportunity to call Legal Aid Ontario now."
üî¥ Possible Section 10(b) violation: CAS continues to question you about your child after you have clearly stated you want to speak with a lawyer first, and before you have had that opportunity.
Section 15
Equality Rights
‚öñÔ∏è
"Every individual is equal before and under the law and has the right to the equal protection and equal benefit of the law without discrimination."
What this means: CAS cannot discriminate against you based on:
‚Ä¢ Race or ethnicity ¬∑ Religion ¬∑ Disability (mental health, addiction, physical)
‚Ä¢ Socioeconomic status ¬∑ Marital status ¬∑ Family structure
‚Ä¢ Indigenous identity (CYFSA Part X adds extra protections)

Poverty is not neglect. Being poor, living in a small home, or receiving social assistance is not a legal ground for child protection involvement under CYFSA s.74(2).

Disability is not bad parenting. A diagnosis of depression, anxiety, ADHD, or other condition does not mean you are unable to parent safely.
üî¥ Possible Section 15 violation: CAS targets families based on racial profiling, removes a child solely because a parent has a mental health diagnosis without assessing actual parenting ability, or applies different standards to Indigenous families without following the CYFSA Part X cultural requirements.
üí° File a complaint: If you believe CAS has discriminated against you, you can file a complaint with the Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario (HRTO) within 1 year. Visit: tribunalsontario.ca/hrto
