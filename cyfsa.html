CYFSA Part I ¬∑ s.1
Guiding Principles ‚Äî The Foundation of Every CAS Decision

Every CAS decision must be guided by these principles ‚Äî in this order:

  • Best interests of the child ‚Äî always the paramount consideration (s.1(1))
  • Least restrictive alternative ‚Äî CAS must use the option that least interferes with family life (s.1(2)(b))
  • Family preservation ‚Äî keeping families together is a goal when safe (s.1(2)(a))
  • Community and cultural connections ‚Äî especially for Indigenous children (s.1(2)(c))
üí° Use this: In court, always ask "Is this the least restrictive option?" CAS must justify why less intrusive alternatives (family support, supervision) won't work before removing a child.
CYFSA s.2
Best Interests ‚Äî All 16 Factors

Courts must consider all 16 best interests factors ‚Äî not just risk. This includes:

  • Physical, mental and emotional needs
  • Level of development and abilities
  • Importance of continuity and stability
  • Relationship with parents, siblings, and extended family
  • Cultural, linguistic, religious and spiritual upbringing
  • Indigenous identity and connection to community
  • Child's own views and wishes
  • Risk of harm if returned or not returned
üí° Defense strategy: Build evidence for factors that favour your parenting ‚Äî child's attachment to you, your cultural role, stability of your home, community support network. A lawyer can argue these 16 factors in your favour.
CYFSA s.74(2)
Child Protection Grounds ‚Äî CAS Must Prove One of These

CAS can only become involved if a child is or is at risk of being in one of these specific situations. Ask CAS: which subsection applies to your case?

s.74(2)(a)
Physical Harm
Child has been or is at risk of being physically harmed by the parent's actions or failure to act.
s.74(2)(b)
Sexual Abuse
Child has been sexually molested or exploited, or is at risk of it.
s.74(2)(c)
Neglect
Child's physical needs are not being met, or the parent fails to provide adequate care or supervision.
s.74(2)(d)
Emotional Harm ‚ö†Ô∏è
Child has suffered or is at risk of serious emotional harm ‚Äî often misused. Emotional harm must be serious and demonstrated, not speculative.
s.74(2)(e)
Mental Incapacity
Parent has a mental, emotional or developmental condition preventing safe care. Diagnosis alone is not enough ‚Äî must affect actual parenting.
s.74(2)(f)
Abandonment
Child has been abandoned by the person having charge of the child.
s.74(2)(g)
Death or Illness of Parent
Parent has died or is unable to care for child through illness and no suitable caregiver is available.
s.74(2)(h)
Consent Agreement Breach
A previous consent agreement or court order is being breached.
CYFSA s.81
Search Warrants ‚Äî When CAS Can Enter Your Home

Under s.81, a justice of the peace can issue a warrant authorizing CAS to enter your premises if satisfied there are reasonable grounds. The warrant must:

  • Be in writing and signed by a justice of the peace
  • Specify the premises to be entered
  • State the grounds for the search
‚ö†Ô∏è Your rights when CAS presents a warrant: Ask to read the warrant carefully. Check: Is your address correct? Is it current (not expired)? Does it specify what they're looking for? You can refuse entry if the warrant is defective or expired.
CYFSA s.84
Emergency Removal Without a Warrant

A child protection worker may remove a child without a warrant only if:

  • There are reasonable grounds to believe a child is in immediate danger
  • There is no time to obtain a warrant without the child being at risk

After emergency removal, the law requires:

  • CAS must bring the child before the court within 5 days (s.84(5))
  • You must be notified immediately of where your child is being held
  • You have the right to a hearing within those 5 days
üö® If your child was removed: Go immediately to the Emergency Protocol page. The 5-day window starts the moment of removal.
CYFSA s.101
Court Orders ‚Äî What a Judge Can Order

After a protection hearing, a court can make the following orders (in order from least to most restrictive):

  • Dismissal ‚Äî no protection finding, child returned, case closed
  • Supervision order ‚Äî child stays home, CAS supervises with conditions
  • Society wardship ‚Äî child in CAS care (up to 12 months), with access rights for parents
  • Crown wardship ‚Äî long-term CAS care, may lead to adoption
üí° Fight for supervision first: A supervision order is the least restrictive option. Your lawyer should always argue for supervision before society wardship.
CYFSA Part X ¬∑ s.216‚Äì226
Indigenous Children ‚Äî Extra Protections

CYFSA Part X provides additional protections for Indigenous children and families:

  • CAS must give notice to the child's band or Native community before any court proceedings
  • The band has the right to participate in protection hearings
  • The court must consider the importance of preserving the child's Indigenous identity and community connection
  • Placement preference: with extended family, then with members of the same Indigenous community, then with other Indigenous families
  • The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) applies
üí° If you are Indigenous: Tell CAS and the court your nation or community immediately. Request that your band be notified. This triggers additional legal protections.

1
Report received: CAS must respond within 12 hours (immediate) to 7 days depending on risk level. You have the right to know a report was made about you.

45-day investigation: Ontario Child Protection Standards require completion within 45 days. During this time: you can refuse home entry, request a lawyer, and request disclosure of the allegations.
